🗣️ B2 Speaking Exam Study Guide

Units 4 & 5: Competition, History, Innovation, and Life Skills

Exam Format: 1 Minute Preparation + 60–90 Seconds Speaking

This guide contains everything you need to succeed in your speaking exam. To get top marks at the B2 (Upper-Intermediate) level, you must do more than just state your answer. You need to express a clear opinion, support it with logical reasons, give specific examples, and use high-level vocabulary.

PART 1: The B2 Speaking Toolkit

Memorize a few phrases from each category below. Use them to structure your 60-90 second speech so that it flows logically and sounds professional.

💡 1. Expressing Your Opinion

Instead of just saying "I think...", use these stronger alternatives:

  • Strong Opinion: "I firmly believe that..." / "I am convinced that..."
  • Considered Opinion: "From my perspective..." / "As far as I am concerned..."
  • Thoughtful Guess: "I reckon that..." / "It seems to me that..."

🏗️ 2. Supporting Your Argument

Always explain the 'why' behind your opinion:

  • "The primary reason for this is..."
  • "This is mainly because..."
  • "Another important factor to consider is..."
  • "Furthermore..." / "Moreover..."

🔍 3. Giving Examples

Connect your opinion to a real-world or historical example:

  • "A prime example of this would be..."
  • "To illustrate this point, consider..."
  • "For instance, if we look at the Space Race..."
  • "Take [the Elgin Marbles / invasive species] as an example..."

⚖️ 4. Acknowledging the Other Side

B2 speakers show they understand both sides before making their point:

  • "While it is true that [competition causes stress], I still believe that..."
  • "Some people might argue that [museums should keep the artifacts], however..."
  • "Despite the obvious benefits of [cooperation], we cannot ignore..."

PART 3: Exam Practice Task

Instructions: Take exactly 1 minute to write down 3-4 bullet points (use the toolkit structures and vocabulary above!). Then, set a timer and speak out loud for 60 to 90 seconds.

PRACTICE CARD

The Digital Compromise

In Unit 4, we saw how technological innovation can solve complex problems. In Unit 5, we explored the fierce debate over who owns historical artifacts like the Elgin Marbles.

Question:

If a museum can create a perfect, interactive 3D hologram of a stolen artifact, should they return the original to its home country and just display the hologram? Or is there a special "legacy" and value in seeing the original, physical object? Justify your opinion.

Pro-Tip for Exam Day:

If you make a grammar mistake while speaking, do not panic. Simply correct yourself quickly and keep going, or just ignore it and focus on fluency. Examiners reward students who can keep speaking smoothly and confidently!
